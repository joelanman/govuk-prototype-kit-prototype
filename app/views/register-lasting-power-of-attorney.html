Overview

A lasting power of attorney (LPA) is a legal document that allows you (as 'the donor') to choose one or more trusted people ('attorneys') to act and make decisions on your behalf.

This guide is also available in Welsh (Cymraeg).

Your LPA will be legally binding only in England and Wales. There’s a different way to make a power of attorney if you need one that:

  • works in Scotland
  • works in Northern Ireland

Unlike a will, an LPA sets out what decisions your attorneys can make while you're alive.

There are 2 types of LPA:

  • personal welfare, for health and care decisions
  • property and affairs, for financial decisions

You can choose to make one type or both.

Both LPAs can be used when they have been registered and when you have lost mental capacity. You can also choose to use the property and affairs LPA while you still have mental capacity.

You can cancel the LPA or remove attorneys at any time, whether it's been registered or not, as long as you have the mental capacity to do so.

The LPA ends when you die.

Your attorneys cannot use your LPA to make changes to your will.

There's a different process in Scotland and process in Northern Ireland.

Why you might make an LPA

An LPA gives you more control over what happens to you if you have an accident or an illness and cannot make your own decisions (you 'lack mental capacity').

If you do not have an LPA and lose the mental capacity to make your own decisions, someone else will need to apply to a court to become your deputy.

Making an LPA instead of having a court appointed deputy:

  • may be quicker
  • costs less money
  • gives you more control

Who makes an LPA

You make your own LPA but you can get help from someone you trust. You can also get help from a professional, like a solicitor or a charity. The decisions you make must be your own.

You must:

  • be aged 18 or over
  • have mental capacity when the LPA is made

You do not need to live in the UK or be a British citizen.

Other people involved in making a lasting power of attorney are your:

  • attorneys - the people you choose to make decisions on your behalf
  • certificate provider - who checks that you understand your LPA and want to make it

How much does an LPA cost

Applying to register an LPA costs £82 unless you get a reduction or exemption.

If you want both a personal welfare LPA and a property and affairs LPA it will cost £164 in total.

If your annual income is under £12,000 you might be eligible for a 50% discount. If you're receiving certain income-related benefits you won't have to pay anything at all.

How long does making an LPA take

When everyone has signed and returned the LPA, there's a 4 week waiting period before it's registered. This is so that people can make objections to the LPA.

Get help from the Office of the Public Guardian

Contact the Office of the Public Guardian (OPG) if you need help deciding if you should make an LPA or if you have any concerns.
